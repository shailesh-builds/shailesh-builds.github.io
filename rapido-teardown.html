01
Strategy canvas · Origin insight

The insight Uber and Ola were too big to see

In 2015, Ola and Uber had been fighting each other for three years over the same battleground — the urban professional who needed an AC car. They were both optimising for the same user, in the same vehicle category, with the same commission-based model. Neither was looking at what was happening between the cars.

Aravind Sanka, one of Rapido's co-founders, had a different observation. Sharing a bike ride to work with a friend saved time, money, and effort. Bikes cut through traffic in ways no car could. The mass-market Indian commuter — travelling 5–10km daily, price-sensitive, perpetually late — had no good option. Auto-rickshaws were unreliable and required haggling. Cabs were too expensive. Public transport was overcrowded.

Rapido's founding insight was not about technology. It was about seeing a user that the incumbents had structurally ignored — and asking why.

"Back in the early days of work, I had realised that sharing a bike ride to work with my friend helped me save quite a bit of money, time, and effort. We beat the traffic every time."
Aravind Sanka, CEO & Co-Founder, Rapido
PM insight — the uncontested market
Rapido did not enter Ola and Uber's market. It created a new one. The bike-taxi segment had no incumbent, no entrenched competitor, and no established expectations. By the time Ola and Uber noticed, Rapido had built a supply base, a user habit, and a brand in a category they had entirely overlooked. This is the classic Blue Ocean move — not competing harder, but competing differently.

Strategy canvas · User lens

Rapido built for a user that Ola and Uber never designed for

Primary rider
The daily commuter — price-sensitive, time-starved
Not the urban professional booking a cab to a meeting. The student, the junior office worker, the delivery agent — travelling 5–8km daily, for whom a ₹50 fare matters and a 2-minute wait time matters more. Uber and Ola priced this user out. Rapido priced them in.
Secondary rider
The traffic escapee — speed over comfort
Bengaluru, Hyderabad, Mumbai — cities where peak-hour traffic is existential. A user who would willingly sacrifice AC comfort to reach somewhere in 12 minutes instead of 40. Rapido's bike arrives faster, leaves faster, and reaches the destination faster. Comfort is not the job. Speed is.
Primary driver — "Captain"
The two-wheeler owner seeking side income
Unlike Ola/Uber which required a commercial four-wheeler — a significant capital investment — Rapido required only what millions of Indians already owned: a motorcycle or scooter. The supply acquisition cost was near zero. The driver (called "Captain") needed no EMI, no fleet owner, no daily rental. Their vehicle was already paid for.
Driver psychology — key insight
Expectations set realistically from day one
Ola and Uber onboarded drivers with promises of ₹1 lakh monthly earnings — then delivered ₹25,000 after commission and expenses. Rapido positioned bike-taxi as a side-income opportunity targeting ₹550–600 per day. Drivers who joined knew exactly what to expect. Lower promises, higher trust, dramatically lower churn.

Strategy canvas · Business model lens

The subscription model that rewrote the rules of Indian ride-hailing

Rapido's most consequential strategic decision was not launching bike taxis. It was abandoning the commission model entirely and replacing it with a flat-fee subscription — before any competitor had considered it a viable option.

Under Ola and Uber's commission model, drivers paid 20–30% of every fare to the platform. On a ₹100 ride, the driver kept ₹70–80 before fuel. On a slow day, this felt extractive. On a ₹30 short ride, it felt punitive. Rapido's SaaS model charged drivers as little as ₹9 per day to access the platform — and let them keep 100% of everything they earned.

01
Low subscription fee attracts more drivers
₹9–29/day vs 20–30% commission. Drivers keep full fare. Supply grows rapidly across cities.
02
More supply means shorter wait times
Denser driver networks reduce rider wait times. Better availability drives higher booking frequency.
03
Lower fares — 10–15% cheaper than rivals
No commission overhead means drivers accept lower fares and still earn more. Price attracts price-sensitive India.
04
More riders join the platform
Lower fares and faster arrivals drive rider adoption. App downloads surpass Uber and Ola in 2024.
05
More rides = more subscription revenue
Platform revenue grows through driver subscriptions and volume — not by taking a cut of each ride.
06
Competitors forced to follow
Uber and Ola abandon their global commission model in India. Rapido has already won the mindshare battle.
The numbers that prove the flywheel worked
Revenue grew 4.4x in two years — from ₹145 crore in FY22 to ₹648 crore in FY24, then to ₹1,000 crore in FY25. Losses fell 45% in FY24 to ₹371 crore. In Q1 FY25, losses narrowed to just ₹17 crore. Rapido is on track to be profitable in FY26. App downloads in 2024: Rapido at 33 million, Uber at 17.7 million, Ola at 17.3 million. The flywheel is self-sustaining.

Strategy canvas · Growth trajectory

How Rapido climbed from bikes to cars — the category expansion playbook

Rapido did not try to compete with Uber and Ola on their home turf from day one. It built its fortress in a category they had ignored, accumulated supply and demand at scale, and only then moved into their territory — already armed with a model they couldn't easily replicate.

2015
Founded as theKarrier — pivots immediately to bike taxis
Aravind Sanka, Pavan Guntupalli, and SR Rishikesh start with a truck aggregation idea, pivot to bike taxis within the year. First-year revenue: ₹6 lakh. Rejected by 75 investors. Launches in Bengaluru.
2016–18
Hero MotoCorp and Rajan Anandan back Rapido — credibility unlocked
Hero MotoCorp Chairman Pawan Munjal and former Google India head Rajan Anandan invest. Rapido gains automotive industry credibility and distribution reach. Expands to Delhi, Hyderabad, and other metros despite regulatory headwinds in several states.
2020–22
Pandemic forces the pivot — logistics fills the gap
Ride-hailing collapses during COVID lockdowns. Rapido pivots aggressively to last-mile logistics and parcel delivery. This keeps the Captain network alive and earning — a decision that builds enormous driver loyalty. Swiggy leads a $180M Series D in 2022, valuing Rapido at $830 million.
2023
The subscription model and cab entry — game over for the old model
Rapido launches the zero-commission subscription model for autos and bikes — inspired by Namma Yatri's proof of concept. Results are immediate: driver satisfaction improves, supply grows, fares fall. In December 2023, Rapido enters cab aggregation with the same subscription model — directly challenging Ola and Uber's core business with a better-aligned economic model. Bike Pink launches for women riders in Chennai.
2024
Unicorn. Uber's CEO names Rapido as India's toughest rival.
Rapido raises $200M in Series E, reaching a $1.1B valuation — unicorn status. Revenue hits ₹648 crore, losses halved. Uber CEO Dara Khosrowshahi says on Nikhil Kamath's podcast: "Ola used to be our main competition. Now, the tougher competition in India is Rapido." Overtakes Uber in Android monthly active users in January 2024 per Sensor Tower. Ola and Uber begin rushing to adopt Rapido's subscription model.
2025
Market leader. ₹1,000 crore. Profitability in sight.
Rapido claims market leadership across all three categories combined. Revenue projected at ₹1,000 crore in FY25. Valuation reaches $2.3B. Launches Ownly — food delivery — taking on Swiggy and Zomato. Targeting 500 cities. Profitable in FY26 as projected. Ola and Uber now operating on Rapido's model — in India at least.

The Ola collapse — cautionary tale
Ola's valuation fell from $7.3 billion in 2021 to $2 billion in 2024 — a 73% destruction of value. The primary cause: founder Bhavish Aggarwal's attention shifted to Ola Electric, leaving the core cab business unfocused and under-invested. Rapido did not beat Ola by being smarter. Ola handed Rapido its market share by being distracted. The lesson for every platform PM: product leadership is a full-time commitment, not a background task.

Strategy canvas · Why it really worked

Five things Rapido understood that Ola and Uber didn't

Insight 01
Supply is the product — not the app
In ride-hailing, the rider experience is entirely determined by supply density. Wait times, fare levels, availability — all of it flows from how many drivers are on the platform. Rapido obsessed over driver economics. When drivers earn more and stay longer, the rider experience improves automatically. Ola and Uber optimised rider UX while neglecting driver economics. Rapido did the opposite — and won both sides.
Insight 02
Asset-light beats capital-intensive at Indian scale
Ola and Uber needed commercial four-wheelers — requiring drivers to take loans, partner with fleet owners, or pay daily rentals. Rapido needed only a two-wheeler the driver already owned. This reduced the supply acquisition cost to near-zero and enabled expansion into 100+ cities, including Tier 2 and Tier 3, that Uber and Ola never reached profitably.
Insight 03
The unserved market is always bigger than the served one
Uber's urban professional user base in India was real but finite. The daily commuter market — hundreds of millions of people making short, frequent trips — was orders of magnitude larger and completely unserved by four-wheelers. Rapido's 2.5 million daily rides are largely trips that Uber and Ola never competed for. These were not stolen customers. They were customers who never existed on a platform before.
Insight 04
Predictability beats maximisation for supply loyalty
Uber and Ola's commission model meant a driver never knew their take-home until the day ended. Rapido's subscription model meant a driver knew their maximum cost from the moment they logged in. Predictability created trust. Trust created retention. Retention created supply density. This is driver psychology understood properly — Rapido built for it. Ola and Uber optimised algorithms instead.
Insight 05
Regulatory headwinds are a moat, not just an obstacle
Bike taxis faced legal bans in Karnataka, Tamil Nadu, and other states for years. Rapido fought these battles, built regulatory relationships, and survived. These same battles deterred well-funded imitators who could have entered the space earlier. By the time regulations stabilised, Rapido had an unassailable head start — supply density, brand recognition, and driver loyalty that no new entrant could quickly replicate.
Insight 06
Category creation beats category entry
Every startup that entered ride-hailing tried to beat Ola and Uber on their terms — same vehicles, same routes, same users, lower prices. All failed. Rapido entered a different game entirely. Creating the bike-taxi category meant there was no incumbent to fight, no price war to sustain, and no network effect to overcome. By the time Ola and Uber noticed, Rapido owned the space.

Strategy canvas · Moat lens

What actually protects Rapido — and what doesn't

Rapido's competitive position is real and hard-earned. But it is not unassailable. Understanding which parts of the moat are durable and which are not is the difference between informed confidence and dangerous complacency.

Strong moat
Bike taxi supply density — 10 years of Captain network
Building 2 million transacting bike-taxi Captains takes years and deep ground-level trust. Any new entrant faces the same chicken-and-egg problem Rapido solved over a decade. This is the hardest thing to replicate quickly — and it is the foundation of every other advantage Rapido has.
Strong moat
Brand as "the bike taxi app" in Indian consciousness
When a rider in Bengaluru or Hyderabad thinks "bike taxi," they think Rapido. This category association — built over a decade — is extraordinarily difficult to dislodge. Uber and Ola entered bike taxis years later. They are "also available in bikes." Rapido is "the bike taxi."
Medium moat
Subscription model — now being copied by every competitor
Rapido's model innovation was real and decisive. But it is no longer proprietary. Ola, Uber, Namma Yatri, and even government-backed Sahkar Taxi all now operate on subscription. The model advantage is eroding. Rapido must compete on execution, not just model innovation going forward.
Medium moat
Cab market position — early but fragile
Rapido's 20–22% cab market share was built quickly after the December 2023 launch. But Uber's 50% share in cabs is backed by a decade of user habit, corporate accounts, and airport presence. Growing from 20% to 40% in cabs is a fundamentally different challenge than growing from 0% to 20%.
The real risk — profitability vs growth tension
Rapido lost ₹371 crore in FY24 despite 46% revenue growth. The subscription model generates predictable but lower per-ride revenue than commissions. Scaling to 500 cities while achieving profitability requires extremely tight cost discipline. Incentive costs — ₹460 crore in FY24 — are the biggest line item and the hardest to reduce while maintaining supply density. If incentives fall, Captains leave. If incentives stay, losses continue. This is the tightrope every marketplace must walk.

Strategy canvas · Change lens

Two things I would prioritise if I were Rapido's PM

Priority 01
Build the Captain ecosystem — not just the ride platform
Rapido's most durable moat is Captain loyalty. But the subscription model, while better than commission, is still transactional. The next level is ecosystem — vehicle maintenance networks, fuel partnerships, insurance products, income smoothing during slow periods, EV transition support. A Captain who earns better, spends less, and builds a livelihood through Rapido is far stickier than one who simply prefers ₹9 to 25% commission. The switch cost from loyalty is much higher than the switch cost from price preference alone.
Priority 02
Own the safety narrative before a major incident forces it
Bike taxis carry an inherent safety perception challenge — especially for women riders. Rapido launched Bike Pink (women-only service with female Captains) in Chennai in 2023, which is the right direction. But safety is not a feature launch — it is a sustained operational commitment. As Rapido expands to 500 cities and scales cabs, one high-profile safety incident could derail the brand faster than any competitive action. Maharashtra's 2025 Aggregator Cabs Policy mandated safety features that Rapido should be building proactively, not reactively. Safety as product, not policy.

Strategy canvas · Overall verdict

What Rapido actually teaches us about disruption

The core lesson
Rapido did not out-execute Ola and Uber. It out-thought them.

Most disruption narratives are about better technology, more funding, or faster execution. Rapido's story is about something more fundamental — seeing a user that the incumbents had structurally ignored, and designing an entire business model around that user's actual economics.

Uber and Ola built for the rider. Rapido built for the driver first — and won the rider as a consequence. When supply is abundant, fares fall, wait times drop, and the rider experience improves automatically. You do not need to optimise the rider experience if you win the supply war.

The subscription model was not just a pricing innovation. It was a signal — to every driver in India — that Rapido was on their side. That signal, repeated consistently over years, built a supply network that no competitor could quickly dismantle. The economic model became a trust model. And trust, in a two-sided marketplace, is the only moat that compounds.

Uber CEO Dara Khosrowshahi naming Rapido as India's toughest rival is not a compliment to Rapido's technology. It is an acknowledgement that Rapido understood the Indian market better than a $140 billion global company — and built for it accordingly. That is what great product thinking looks like in practice.

The PM reading from this teardown

The most important question any product team can ask is not "how do we beat the competition?" It is "who is the user nobody is designing for?"

Ola and Uber had enormous advantages — capital, brand, technology, regulatory experience. Rapido had none of these. What it had was a cleaner answer to the question of who it was building for. A bike-owner seeking side income. A student needing a ₹40 ride to college. A commuter willing to trade comfort for speed. None of these users appeared in Ola or Uber's personas. All of them appeared in Rapido's founding insight.

The other reading: supply is strategy, not operations. In any two-sided marketplace, the side that is harder to acquire and retain is the real product. For ride-hailing in India, that was always the driver. Rapido built its entire product philosophy around that truth. Ola and Uber are still catching up.
